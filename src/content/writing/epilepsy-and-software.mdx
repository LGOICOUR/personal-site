There's an obvious gap between the things you do as a PhD student
modeling a disease and the things you do as a developer shipping
consumer software. Different time horizons, different
stakeholders, different definitions of "done."

I want to argue the opposite, though — that the most useful
disciplines I built during my thesis were not about epilepsy or
even about modeling. They were about how to maintain reliable
beliefs about a system you can only partially observe.

## Three habits that ported

The first is **assume your instruments lie until proven
otherwise**. In a PhD modeling project, a "result" is one
hyperparameter choice away from being noise. In software, a green
test suite is one mock away from being theater. The instinct to
ask *what would I have to believe for this measurement to be
trustworthy* applies equally to a seizure detector and to a
metrics dashboard.

The second is **the smallest reproducible case is the unit of
work**. In modeling, you can spend a week on a result that turns
out to depend on a particular random seed. The fix is to refuse
to claim anything you can't reproduce from scratch. In software
that's the bug repro; in modeling it's the minimal example
notebook. Same discipline.

The third is **write things down for the version of you that
shows up six months later**. Past-you and future-you are
basically strangers. Modeling forced me to keep careful lab
notebooks; shipping software has required me to keep careful
change logs and ADRs. The artifact is different; the habit is
identical.

## What didn't port

A lot, in fairness.

- Calibration on long timescales. A modeling project is patient
  in a way that consumer software simply isn't.
- The freedom to pursue a question that isn't on anyone's
  roadmap. I miss it.
- The mental model of "results" as something you publish once
  and defend. Software is closer to gardening than to publishing.

But the habits in the first list have been my most reliable
inheritance from grad school. They're the parts I still use
every week.
